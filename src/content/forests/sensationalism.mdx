climate: |
  In this forest, movement feels like proof. God seems closest when the room is loud, the testimony is dramatic, the worship swells, and the language burns hot. Silence does not feel peaceful here. It feels like withdrawal, distance, or hidden failure.

  What feels normal is urgency, visible power, emotional escalation, and constant expectation of the next breakthrough. What feels dangerous is stillness, patience, ordinary obedience, or any call to test what is happening. Faith, when it appears, feels like responsiveness: staying open, stirred, and ready for something explosive.
canopy:
  - ordinary faithfulness becomes hard to trust.
  - discernment becomes hard to practice.
  - weakness and hiddenness become hard to honor.
  - the slow work of formation becomes hard to notice.
quietGospel: "If God is really here, it will feel intense."
fruit:
  - emotional dependence on charged experiences
  - suspicion toward quiet obedience
  - platform charisma mistaken for spiritual authority
  - discernment weakened by urgency
  - exhaustion masked as zeal
costStaying: |
  Over time, the soul can become unable to recognize God unless something dramatic is happening. Prayer starts to need atmosphere. Worship starts to need escalation. Scripture starts to feel thin unless it arrives through a personality, a platform, or a moment.

  You may become very responsive and not very rooted. Disappointment grows, but because the culture rewards intensity, the ache gets covered with more noise.
costLeaving: |
  Leaving can feel like betraying the Spirit, losing tenderness, or becoming cold and cynical. It may also mean grieving relationships built around shared fire, language, and expectation.

  You may fear that if you step away from the storm, you are stepping away from God Himself.
pathIntro: |
  The following reflections do not argue against the living God, the gifts of the Spirit, or genuine wonder. They begin by staying close to the reader inside this forest, naming why intensity can feel like life and why quiet can feel frightening.

  From there, they slowly draw a distinction between holy fire and religious adrenaline, between spiritual hunger and manipulation, and between Christ's real presence and the pressure to keep an experience alive.
orientation: |
  You do not have to denounce every intense moment to begin telling the truth.

  But you do need permission to ask whether intensity has become your interpreter, and whether the Spirit may be leading you toward something steadier, truer, and deeper than spiritual escalation.
